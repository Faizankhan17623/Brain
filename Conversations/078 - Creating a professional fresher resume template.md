Faizan is asking me to explain what should and shouldn't go in a professional summary for a fresher resume. Let me give him clear, practical guidance.Great question. Let me break this down clearly for you — think of this as a rulebook you can follow every time you tweak the summary for a different JD.

**What TO write in a Professional Summary:**

Your role title should come first — "Full Stack Developer" or whatever the JD says. This is the first thing ATS scans for, and the recruiter's eyes land here. If the JD says "Junior Software Engineer," your summary should open with that exact title.

Your core tech stack comes next, and always expand abbreviations the first time. Write "MERN stack (MongoDB, Express.js, React.js, Node.js)" not just "MERN stack" — ATS might search for "React.js" separately and miss it if you only wrote "MERN."

Mention what you actually built, not what you "know." There's a massive difference between "knowledge of payment gateway integration" and "integrated Razorpay payment gateway with webhook validation." The second one proves you did it. Recruiters see hundreds of summaries that say "knowledge of React" — yours should say "built and deployed two production-grade applications."

Include one or two specific technical highlights that most freshers won't have. For you, that's "atomic MongoDB transactions" and "automated ticketing via cron jobs." These are differentiators — they make the recruiter stop and think "this guy actually built something real."

Your education, but only if it strengthens your profile. Your 8.10 GPA does, so include it. If your GPA was 6.0, I'd say skip it.

Availability — "available to join immediately" removes a logistical question from the recruiter's mind. It's a small thing but it matters when they're comparing 50 resumes.

**What NOT to write:**

Never write soft skill claims without proof. "Hard-working," "team player," "quick learner," "passionate developer" — these are completely meaningless on paper. Every single fresher writes these. A recruiter has literally no way to verify "I'm a quick learner" from a resume. Your projects already prove you learn fast — let them do the talking.

Never use "eager," "aspiring," "seeking opportunity," or "looking for a chance." These make you sound like you're begging. You're not asking for charity — you're offering skills in exchange for a salary. There's a big difference between "eager to contribute in a team environment" and "available to join immediately."
