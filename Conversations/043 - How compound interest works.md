## How Compound Interest Works

**Simple interest** earns interest only on your original principal.
**Compound interest** earns interest on your principal *plus* all the interest you've already earned — so your interest starts earning interest. That's the magic.

### The formula

```
A = P × (1 + r)^t
```

- **P** = principal (what you start with)
- **r** = annual interest rate (as a decimal)
- **t** = time in years
- **A** = final amount

---

### A concrete example — ₹10,000 at 8% for 20 years

| Year | Simple Interest | Compound Interest |
|------|----------------|-------------------|
| 0    | ₹10,000        | ₹10,000           |
| 5    | ₹14,000        | ₹14,693           |
| 10   | ₹18,000        | ₹21,589           |
| 15   | ₹22,000        | ₹31,722           |
| 20   | ₹26,000        | **₹46,610**       |

With simple interest you earn ₹16,000 in gains. With compound interest you earn **₹36,610** — more than double — on the exact same money.

---

### Why the curve bends upward

In year 1, you earn ₹800 in interest. But in year 10, you earn ~₹1,600 — twice as much, because now you're earning 8% on a much larger base. Every year, the amount of interest generated gets bigger and bigger. This is the **exponential curve** — slow and unremarkable at first, then dramatic near the end.

---

### The Rule of 72

A quick mental math trick: divide **72 by your interest rate** to find roughly how many years it takes to double your money.

- At 8% → 72 ÷ 8 = **~9 years** to double
- At 12% → 72 ÷ 12 = **~6 years** to double

---

### Key takeaway

The two things that turbocharge compound interest are **rate** and **time** — and time is the most powerful. Starting 10 years earlier can matter more than doubling your investment amount.
